I am going to speak about resilience

Resilience against adoption

Testimonial text prepared by Olmo Gómez Aldaz for the open panel “Different voices”, within the conference “Paths of Resilience”, XV Anniversary of La Voz de los Adoptados, held on 20 June 2026 at the Ministry of Youth and Children, Madrid.

The text presents resilience from a concrete experience of failed adoption, recovery of birth identity, judicial recognition of biological filiation, dual filiation, democratic memory and an abolitionist position against adoption.

Context

I am going to speak about resilience was prepared as an intervention for a public space dedicated to different voices within the field of adoption. Its starting point is a lived experience of failed adoption, search for origin and recovery of birth identity.

The text is situated within a personal and public trajectory marked by identity abduction, irregular adoption, genetic evidence, judicial restitution of biological filiation, dual filiation and recognition as a victim within the framework of democratic memory.

The intervention turns that experience into public speech. The testimony articulates a critical position on adoption as an institution capable of replacing identity, erasing origin, imposing belonging and organising a life around a legal fiction.

Approach of the text

The central axis of the text is the idea of resilience against adoption. Resilience appears as an exit from the adoptive narrative, as recovery of birth identity, defence of name and filiation, and the ability to sustain a truth that had been denied for decades.

The intervention shifts the usual narrative that presents adoption as reparation. It speaks from an adoption that produced harm, erasure and substitution. From there, resilience is linked to the recovery of origin, legal action, recognition of biological filiation and critical elaboration of what was lived.

The text also formulates a critique of adoptive Stockholm syndrome: the emotional, symbolic and narrative dependency that can bind an adopted person to the structure that replaced their identity. Leaving that narrative appears as part of the process of restitution.

The conclusion of the text is abolitionist: adoption must be abolished and replaced by forms of care, protection and guardianship that guarantee stability without erasing identity or manufacturing family fictions.

Main themes

Resilience against adoption: the text presents resilience as a force directed towards leaving the adoptive narrative, recovering origin and reconstructing birth identity.

Failed adoption: the intervention speaks from an experience in which adoption produced harm, identity substitution and a life organised around a documentary fiction.

Birth identity: the recovery of origin appears as a vital, documentary and judicial process affecting name, surnames, filiation and family belonging.

Dual filiation: the text is linked to the judicial recognition of biological filiation with full effects, while adoptive filiation still remains.

Stolen babies and democratic memory: the personal experience is inscribed within a historical framework of newborn abduction, irregular adoptions and institutional recognition of victims.

Abolition of adoption: the intervention defends forms of protection, care and permanent guardianship that preserve birth identity and continuity with origin.
