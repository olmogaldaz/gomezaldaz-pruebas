My most personal interview

Published in El Observatorio no. 9 under the title “The battle for my identity”, this interview goes through my personal, family and legal history: the search for origins, the reunion with my biological sister, the DNA test, the recovery of my biological filiation and the harm caused by identity abduction and fraudulent adoption.

The interview arose from a visit to the Pinoduero Pre-employment Workshop in Tudela de Duero, where I shared my story, presented my book and explained the process of searching, restoring identity and obtaining judicial recognition.

It is one of the most complete and personal interviews I have given so far. It includes not only the documentary and judicial facts of the case, but also the intimate experience of the search, loneliness, psychological harm, lack of recognition and the emotional weight of having had to reconstruct my own identity.

What this interview contains

The interview explains how I discovered my adoption, how I began searching for my origins and how I found documents, silences, contradictions and traces of a network linked to the Maria Madre Association of Bilbao.

It also addresses the role of Mercedes Herrán de Gras, my initial status as an abandoned child, the missing or destroyed documentation, the irregular registration, the search for my biological family and the process that finally confirmed through DNA my bond with my sister Amaia.

The piece places my case within a broader reality: stolen babies, irregular adoptions, concealment of filiations, identity substitution and the lack of effective reparation for victims.

Main topics

  • Search for origins and reconstruction of personal identity.
  • Fraudulent adoption, identity abduction and biological filiation.
  • Maria Madre Association of Bilbao and the network of irregular adoptions.
  • Reunion with the biological family and confirmation through DNA testing.
  • Psychological harm, loneliness, institutional silence and lack of recognition.
  • Judicial ruling, partial restitution of identity and the right to truth.

Documentary and personal value

This interview has documentary value because it publicly summarises an essential part of my case: the search, the evidence, the judicial recognition and the historical dimension of an identity abduction produced within the system of irregular adoptions in Spain.

But it also has special personal value. It is not a cold or merely informative interview. It was made from a local, community and human space, with questions that made it possible to speak about aspects that rarely appear in judicial texts or news reports.

That is why I consider it my most personal interview: because it does not reduce my story to a legal case, but allows the human journey to be seen of someone who had to reconstruct his identity after a life built on a false filiation.
